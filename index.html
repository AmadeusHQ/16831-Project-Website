A Comparative Study of Different RL Algorithms on Unitree Go2 Robot

Yinong He 1, Xinhe Wang 1, Yutong Wang 1
1 Carnegie Mellon University
Paper Code

Abstract

We present a comparative study of reinforcement learning algorithms for velocity-tracking locomotion of the Unitree Go2 quadruped on rough terrain, using the Isaac-Velocity-Rough-Unitree-Go2-v0 environment from IsaacLab. We benchmark three baseline methods — on-policy PPO, off-policy SAC, and model-based TD-MPC2 — and study two modifications to the standard setup. The first replaces joint position control with direct joint torque control, exposing the policy to the full nonlinear, contact-rich dynamics of locomotion. The second introduces three variants of Group-Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO) — fixed-bin GRPO, Adaptive-GRPO, and Hard-Sync GRPO — that replace the global value baseline with a command-conditional one. PPO with position control achieves the best overall tracking accuracy, while Hard-Sync GRPO yields the fastest early-phase convergence among the GRPO variants. Torque-control policies improve substantially over the random baseline but still trail their position-control counterparts, highlighting the difficulty of jointly learning balance, contact handling, and velocity tracking from torques alone.

Method

Environment

We use the Isaac-Velocity-Rough-Unitree-Go2-v0 environment from IsaacLab [1], in which a Unitree Go2 quadruped learns to track velocity commands on rough terrain. The 48-D observation concatenates base linear and angular velocity (3+3), projected gravity in the robot frame (3), the target command (vxcmd, vycmd, ωzcmd) (3), joint positions and velocities of all 12 joints (12+12), and a height scan of the local terrain. The 12-D action specifies joint position targets for the hip, thigh, and calf of each of the four legs.

Reward Function

We design the reward to encourage tracking of commanded planar linear velocity and yaw rate, while penalizing unstable vertical motion and inefficient or non-smooth actuation. The tracking errors are

elin = ‖vxy − vxycmd‖2,    eang = |ωz − ωzcmd|.

These errors are converted into bounded tracking rewards via exponential kernels:

rlin = exp(−elin2/σlin2),    rang = exp(−eang2/σang2),

which yields smooth gradients and keeps each term in (0, 1]. To discourage instability and excessive actuation, we also include penalties on vertical velocity, joint torques, and joint accelerations:

pz = vz2,    pτ = ‖τ‖22,    pq̈ = ‖q̈‖22.

The per-timestep reward is then a weighted combination rt = wlin rlin + wang rang − wz pz − wτ pτ − wq̈ pq̈, trading off tracking against stability, energy efficiency, and smoothness.

Modification A: Torque-Control Policy

In the baseline setup, the action space is 12 joint position targets, tracked by an internal PD controller. While this simplifies learning, it abstracts away the low-level actuation dynamics. In Modification A, the policy directly outputs joint torques at ∈ ℝ12. Removing the implicit PD controller exposes the full nonlinear, contact-rich dynamics of quadruped locomotion: the policy must regulate forces, maintain balance, and handle impacts purely through torque outputs. This converts the problem from trajectory generation into full dynamic control, providing a more rigorous test of how each RL algorithm handles low-level continuous control and contact instability.

Modification B: GRPO and Variants

The policy is conditioned on commanded velocities ct = (vxcmd, vycmd, ωzcmd). Different commands induce locomotion tasks of very different difficulty, so a global value baseline incurs high gradient variance. Group-Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO) [2] replaces the global baseline with a command-conditional one by partitioning rollouts into groups g of similar commands and using the group-relative advantage

Ai,tGR = Ri,t − R̄g,    R̄g = (1/|g|) Σ(i,τ)∈g Ri,τ,

optionally normalized by std(Rg). The policy is updated with a clipped policy-gradient objective and a KL regularizer to πθold; no additional value network is required. We study three variants:

  • GRPO (fixed bins). Discretize each component of ct into a fixed number of bins and hash ct into the resulting Cartesian grid.
  • Adaptive-GRPO. Construct groups dynamically each iteration via K-means in command space, removing the need to hand-tune bin boundaries and keeping group sizes roughly balanced.
  • Hard-Sync GRPO. Eliminate residual intra-group heterogeneity by partitioning the N parallel environments into N/G fixed groups of size G; at each command resample we draw a single ct(g) per group and broadcast it to all members. On rough terrain we additionally synchronize the curriculum so all group members share the same sub-terrain. The empirical group mean R̄g then becomes an unbiased Monte-Carlo estimate of the exact condition-conditional value.

Algorithms Compared

We compare three baseline RL methods — on-policy PPO [3], off-policy SAC [4], and model-based TD-MPC2 [5] — under position control, plus PPO and SAC trained with torque control (Modification A), and the three GRPO variants (Modification B).

Analysis

PPO with position control achieves the strongest overall performance, with the lowest elin = 0.2687 m/s and eang = 0.1519 rad/s. Its on-policy updates and clipped objective provide stable improvement and smooth locomotion control. SAC learns useful behavior under position control but converges more slowly and tracks less accurately than PPO; its entropy coefficient α decays rapidly, limiting exploration and pushing the policy toward a stationary, balance-first local optimum. TD-MPC2 reaches competitive returns intermittently but exhibits large checkpoint-to-checkpoint variance (e.g. elin swings between 0.479 and 1.114 in late training), which we attribute to model errors compounding through the latent planner on contact-rich rough terrain.

Torque control is consistently harder than position control: removing the implicit PD controller forces the policy to learn balance, contact handling, and velocity tracking simultaneously. PPO-torque drops to elin = 0.7307 m/s while keeping angular tracking close to PPO-position; SAC-torque hugs the random baseline for most of training, with frequent falls in qualitative rollouts.

Hard-Sync GRPO converges remarkably fast — reaching a return of ≈16 within ~15k environment steps and plateauing in the 15–17 range — clearly dominating fixed-bin GRPO and Adaptive-GRPO, which are still rising at the 30k cutoff. Its elin = 0.4653 m/s and eang = 0.2236 rad/s beat TD-MPC2 on linear tracking and match it on angular, while remaining more stable across late checkpoints. Hard-Sync GRPO trades a small amount of asymptotic accuracy for substantially better early-phase sample efficiency, despite using no critic network.

Conclusion and Future Work

Conclusion

We compared on-policy PPO, off-policy SAC, model-based TD-MPC2, and three GRPO variants on the Unitree Go2 rough-terrain velocity-tracking task, under both position and torque control. PPO with position control achieved the strongest and most stable overall performance, reaching the highest return, the lowest velocity-tracking errors, and the smoothest learning curve. SAC was able to learn useful locomotion under position control but improved more slowly and was less stable; TD-MPC2 sometimes reached competitive performance but suffered from large instability driven by model errors in the contact-rich terrain. The two modifications add complementary insights: torque control makes the task substantially harder because the policy must directly learn balance, contact handling, and velocity tracking, while Hard-Sync GRPO provided very fast early learning and stable behavior with no critic network, although its final accuracy stayed slightly below PPO.

Future Work

Future work should focus on improving the trade-off between fast learning and final performance. One direction is to strengthen exploration in SAC — for example by regularizing or scheduling the entropy coefficient α so it does not collapse early — to avoid the stationary, balance-first local optimum we observed. Another is to stabilize TD-MPC2 by improving the learned dynamics model and planner settings, since its performance fluctuates heavily when the latent model mispredicts contact-rich interactions. Finally, both Hard-Sync GRPO and torque-control policies merit further development: Hard-Sync GRPO learns very quickly but does not yet match PPO's final accuracy, and torque control is more realistic and flexible but makes balance and contact handling much harder. Improving these methods could yield policies that are both sample-efficient and robust in more realistic locomotion settings.
